What is a breadboard?

A breadboard is a piece of hardware that allows someone to create, test, and debug computer circuts before putting it on a miylar board. They consist of a array of pins, power, input pins, output pins and ground pins. Take note of the image in figure 1.1.

Power pins give a +5 volts to any wires or other devices conected to them.
Ground pins are grounded and are needed to complete a circut.
Dip Switches labeled 1 to 8 toggle the power flow through the input pins A to F respectively.
Input pins supply a +5V when the corrasponding dipswitch is on. The LED located above each one lets you know if that group of input pins has power.
Output pins are grounded and turn on when a power is supplyed to them. Pin array is where all your circutry is done. It incorperates the power, input, output, and ground pins.

The pin array can be broken down even further into the top rail, the top half, the gap, the bottom half, and the bottom rail (Figure 1.2).

The top and bottom rails are connented horizontaly. So if power is supplied to any pin in them they all are connected. You generaly connect the +5V to the top rail and the ground to the bottom rail. From there you would distrubute the power.
The top half and bottom half are different. Their pins are connected vertically and are separated by the "Gap". This means that if power is given to a pin in the top half all the pins vertically from that pin in the top half would then have power.
The gap is a very useful part of the breadboard. It is the right space for a chip to be inserted (it also is the only place the "Virtual Breadboard" will let you put it). Since the top and bottom half's pins are connected vertically it allows you to control each pin individualy.
